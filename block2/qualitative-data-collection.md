# Qualitative Data Collection Methods

## Interviews

Interviews are the most common qualitative data collection method in LIS research. They range from highly structured (predetermined questions in fixed order) to unstructured (conversational), with *semi-structured* interviews being the most common choice for dissertation research.

### Semi-Structured Interview Design Principles

Drawing on Glesne (2016) and Merriam and Tisdell (2016):

- Begin with **rapport-building** questions that are easy to answer
- Move from general to specific (the "funnel" approach)
- Use **open-ended questions**: "Tell me about...", "How did you experience...", "What was it like when..."
- Prepare **probes** for follow-up: "Can you tell me more about that?", "What do you mean by...?", "Can you give me an example?"
- Avoid leading questions, double-barreled questions, and jargon
- End with a **catch-all** question: "Is there anything else you think I should know about...?"

### Practical Considerations

- Audio/video recording with informed consent
- Interview length (typically 45–90 minutes for in-depth interviews)
- Location (comfortable, private, convenient for participant)
- Building and maintaining rapport while remaining appropriately neutral
- Pilot testing with 1–2 participants before full data collection

```{tip}
Your interview guide is a *guide*, not a script. The semi-structured format means you should be prepared to follow interesting leads while ensuring all key topics are covered. Number your questions but remain flexible in ordering.
```

## Focus Groups

Breen (2006) outlines a practical approach to focus group research:

- **Optimal size**: 6–8 participants per group
- **Number of groups**: Typically 3–5, continuing until saturation
- **Moderator role**: Facilitating discussion, ensuring all voices are heard, managing dominant participants
- **Guide structure**: Opening question → Introductory question → Transition questions → Key questions → Ending question

```{warning}
Focus groups produce *group-level* data, not individual-level data. They are excellent for exploring shared understandings and group dynamics, but not for deeply personal or sensitive topics where participants might self-censor.
```

## Observation and Fieldwork

Emerson, Fretz, and Shaw (2011) provide the foundational framework for ethnographic observation and fieldnote writing:

**Jottings**
: Brief notes taken in the field to capture key moments, phrases, and sensory details

**Fieldnotes**
: Detailed written accounts produced as soon as possible after observation, transforming jottings into rich description

**Descriptive vs. Analytic notes**
: Separate what you *see* from what you *think about* what you see

### Observer Roles (Gold, 1958)

A continuum from detachment to immersion:

1. **Complete observer**-No interaction with participants
2. **Observer as participant**-Primarily observing, with some interaction
3. **Participant as observer**-Primarily participating, with observation role known
4. **Complete participant**-Full immersion, observation role may be unknown

## Document and Archival Analysis

Bowen (2009) establishes document analysis as a legitimate standalone method:

- Systematic procedure for reviewing and evaluating documents
- Combines elements of content analysis and thematic analysis
- Requires attention to the *context of production*-who created the document, for whom, and why?
- Particularly relevant for LIS researchers studying archival materials, institutional records, and policy documents

```{important}
Documents are not neutral artifacts. They were produced for particular purposes by particular people in particular contexts. Your analysis must account for this-not just extracting content, but interpreting documents as social products.
```

## Trustworthiness in Data Collection

Glesne (2016) synthesizes trustworthiness strategies that should be built *into* your data collection plan, not bolted on afterward:

```{list-table} Trustworthiness Strategies
:header-rows: 1

* - Strategy
  - Description
* - **Prolonged engagement**
  - Extended time in the field for observations, interviews, and interaction
* - **Triangulation**
  - Multiple data sources, methods, investigators, or theories
* - **Rich, thick description**
  - Detailed descriptive writing that allows readers to understand context
* - **Negative case analysis**
  - Actively seeking evidence that contradicts your emerging patterns
* - **Member checking**
  - Sharing transcripts or findings with participants for verification
* - **Peer debriefing**
  - External review of your interpretive process
* - **Audit trail**
  - Systematic documentation of all research decisions
* - **Reflexivity**
  - Ongoing critical self-reflection on researcher positionality
```

## ✏️ Exercise: Build Your Data Collection Protocol

Create a *complete* protocol document for your primary data collection method:

**If conducting interviews:**
- Full interview guide with 8–12 main questions and probes for each
- Opening and closing scripts
- Estimated timing for each section
- Recording and transcription plan

**If conducting observations:**
- Observation protocol template (what to record, how often, how long)
- Fieldnote format and schedule
- Researcher positionality statement

**If analyzing documents/archives:**
- Data extraction form or codebook
- Inclusion/exclusion criteria for documents
- Sampling strategy for document selection
