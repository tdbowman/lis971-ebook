# Digital Methods and Research Ethics

## Digital Trace Data

Salganik (2018) and Freelon (2014) address the unique opportunities and challenges of collecting data from digital platforms.

### Found Data vs. Designed Data

Salganik (2018) draws a crucial distinction:

**Found data** (readymade)
: Data that already exist — tweets, forum posts, library circulation records, web logs. Not created for research purposes.

**Designed data** (custommade)
: Data deliberately collected for research — surveys, experiments, interviews conducted online.

Most digital trace data are *found data*, which carries both advantages and challenges.

### Characteristics of Digital Trace Data

```{list-table}
:header-rows: 1

* - Characteristic
  - Advantage
  - Challenge
* - **Non-reactive**
  - Not influenced by researcher presence
  - May not capture what you actually need
* - **Large-scale**
  - Statistical power; rare event detection
  - Noise; spurious patterns
* - **Always-on**
  - Continuous data streams
  - Overwhelming volume
* - **Incomplete**
  - —
  - Missing demographics; platform-specific populations
```

### Interpreting Digital Trace Data

Freelon (2014) warns against treating digital trace data as transparent records of behavior. Digital traces are:

- **Platform-mediated**: The platform's design shapes what data are produced
- **Algorithmically filtered**: What you can see may not be what exists
- **Context-dependent**: The same action (e.g., a "like") means different things in different contexts
- **Temporally unstable**: Platforms change; data collected today may not be comparable to data collected last year

## Research Ethics

### Core Ethical Principles

The Belmont Report (1979) establishes three foundational principles:

1. **Respect for persons**: Individuals are autonomous agents; those with diminished autonomy require additional protections. Operationalized through *informed consent*.
2. **Beneficence**: Maximize benefits and minimize risks. Operationalized through *risk-benefit analysis*.
3. **Justice**: Fair distribution of research burdens and benefits. Operationalized through *equitable selection* of participants.

### Digital Research Ethics

Zimmer (2010) challenges the assumption that publicly available digital data can be used without ethical scrutiny:

**Contextual integrity**
: Data shared in one context (e.g., a health support forum) may not have been intended for research scrutiny. Just because data are *accessible* does not mean they are *appropriate* to use without consent.

**Re-identification risk**
: Even "anonymized" data can often be re-identified through triangulation with other datasets.

**Platform terms of service**
: May or may not align with research ethics requirements. Compliance with ToS does not equal ethical research practice.

**Power asymmetries**
: Researchers accessing community data without community benefit or input raises justice concerns.

```{important}
"But the data is already public" is not an ethical argument. Zimmer (2010) demonstrates that public availability does not automatically exempt data from ethical review. Your IRB application should explicitly address how you will handle digital data ethically.
```

### Computational Social Science

Lazer et al. (2009) outline the emerging field of computational social science, noting both its transformative potential and its ethical challenges. Key considerations for LIS researchers:

- Algorithmic transparency: Can you explain how your computational methods work?
- Reproducibility: Can others replicate your computational analysis?
- Bias: Do your data and methods systematically exclude or misrepresent certain groups?

## Writing Your Ethics Section

Your methodology chapter should address:

- **IRB review status**: Exempt, expedited, or full review — and why
- **Informed consent procedures**: Written, verbal, or waiver — and justification
- **Confidentiality protections**: Pseudonyms, de-identification, data security
- **Risk mitigation strategies**: What are the risks and how will you minimize them?
- **Participant compensation**: If any — and why or why not
- **Data retention and disposal**: How long will you keep data? How will you destroy it?

```{tip}
Write your ethics section *before* submitting your IRB application. The exercise of articulating your ethical procedures for your methodology chapter will help you complete the IRB application more effectively.
```
