# Quantitative Instruments and Protocols

## Survey Design Principles

Dillman, Smyth, and Christian (2016) offer the *Tailored Design Method* — a comprehensive approach to maximizing survey response quality.

### Question Design

- Use simple, specific language
- Avoid double-barreled questions (asking two things at once)
- Minimize social desirability bias through careful wording
- Match response categories to the question being asked
- Use balanced scales (equal positive and negative options)

### Response Scales

**Likert-type scales** (typically 5 or 7 points)
: "Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree." Odd-numbered scales include a neutral midpoint; even-numbered scales force a direction.

**Semantic differential scales**
: Bipolar adjective pairs (e.g., "Easy ——— Difficult"). Useful for measuring attitudes and perceptions.

**Frequency scales**
: "Never" to "Always." Be specific about the reference period ("In the past 30 days, how often did you...").

### Survey Mode Considerations

- **Online surveys** (Qualtrics, Formbricks): Convenient, cost-effective, but potential coverage error
- **Mail surveys**: Higher response rates for some populations, but slow and expensive
- **Mixed-mode designs**: Can reduce coverage error but introduce mode effects

## Using Existing Instruments vs. Developing New Ones

### When to Use Existing Instruments

- Established validity and reliability evidence
- Allows comparison with prior research
- Saves considerable development time

### When to Develop New Instruments

- No existing instrument measures your construct
- Existing instruments are not appropriate for your population
- Your research explores a novel construct

```{warning}
If you adapt or modify an existing instrument, you must re-establish its psychometric properties with your population. Reliability and validity evidence from the original study does not automatically transfer.
```

## Reliability and Validity

### Reliability (Consistency of Measurement)

**Internal consistency** (Cronbach's alpha)
: Measures how well items in a scale measure the same construct. Convention: α ≥ 0.70 is acceptable (Nunnally & Bernstein, 1994).

**Test-retest reliability**
: Administering the same instrument twice to the same group and correlating the results. Measures stability over time.

**Inter-rater reliability**
: Degree of agreement among multiple coders or raters. Essential for content analysis and observational coding schemes.

### Validity (Measuring What You Intend)

**Content validity**
: Does the instrument cover the full domain of the construct? Typically established through expert review.

**Construct validity**
: Does the instrument relate to other measures as theory predicts? Includes convergent and discriminant validity.

**Criterion validity**
: Does the instrument predict relevant outcomes? Includes concurrent and predictive validity.

## Data Management Planning

Corti (2014) emphasizes that data management is integral to research design, not an afterthought:

**File naming conventions and version control**
: Establish a consistent system before you begin collecting data. Include date stamps, version numbers, and descriptive labels.

**Data storage and backup**
: The 3-2-1 rule — 3 copies, 2 media types, 1 offsite. Use encrypted storage for sensitive data.

**Documentation**
: Codebooks, variable definitions, data dictionaries. Your future self (and your committee) will thank you.

**Data security**
: Encryption, access controls, de-identification procedures. Essential for IRB compliance.

**Retention and sharing**
: Know your disciplinary norms, funder requirements, and IRB protocols for data retention and potential sharing.

## ✏️ Exercise: Build Your Quantitative Protocol

If you are using quantitative data collection methods:

1. **Instrument selection**: Identify the specific instrument(s) you will use. If existing, cite the original validation study. If new, describe your development plan.
2. **Psychometric evidence**: Report reliability and validity data from prior studies (if using existing instruments) or your pilot testing plan (if developing new ones).
3. **Administration procedures**: How, when, and where will data be collected? What is your distribution strategy?
4. **Data management plan**: File naming, storage, backup, security, and retention.
