# Choosing and Defending Your Research Design

## The Three Research Approaches

Creswell and Creswell (2023) organize the landscape of social science inquiry into three broad approaches — qualitative, quantitative, and mixed methods — each associated with distinct philosophical assumptions, design types, and methods.

```{list-table} Qualitative, Quantitative, and Mixed Methods Approaches
:header-rows: 1
:widths: 15 28 28 29

* - Dimension
  - Qualitative
  - Quantitative
  - Mixed Methods
* - **Philosophy**
  - Constructivist / Transformative
  - Postpositivist
  - Pragmatic
* - **Designs**
  - Phenomenology, Grounded Theory, Ethnography, Case Study, Narrative
  - Experimental, Quasi-experimental, Correlational, Survey
  - Convergent, Explanatory Sequential, Exploratory Sequential
* - **Data**
  - Open-ended interviews, observations, documents, images
  - Closed-ended surveys, instruments, performance data
  - Both open- and closed-ended
* - **Analysis**
  - Thematic coding, narrative analysis, constant comparison
  - Statistical procedures, hypothesis testing
  - Integration of qualitative themes and quantitative results
* - **Quality**
  - Trustworthiness, transferability, confirmability
  - Validity, reliability, generalizability
  - Both sets + integration quality
```

## The Logic of Design Choice

Your research design is not a preference — it is an *argument*. The design must be justified by:

1. **The nature of your research problem**: Exploratory problems (understanding lived experience, generating theory) call for qualitative designs. Explanatory or predictive problems (testing relationships, measuring outcomes) call for quantitative designs. Complex problems requiring both breadth and depth call for mixed methods.

2. **Your research questions**: Questions beginning with "how" or "what" (in an exploratory sense) lean qualitative. Questions about relationships, differences, or effects lean quantitative. Questions requiring both types of evidence lean mixed methods.

3. **Your worldview**: Your philosophical assumptions should align with your design choice (see {doc}`philosophical-foundations`).

4. **Practical constraints**: Access to participants, timeline, resources, and your own methodological expertise all legitimately factor into design decisions.

```{important}
Your methodology chapter must justify the *alignment* between your research questions, worldview, and chosen design. A committee will ask: "Why this design and not another?"
```

## Qualitative Research Designs in Depth

Maxwell (2013) offers a particularly useful *interactive model* of qualitative research design, emphasizing that qualitative design is not linear but involves ongoing negotiation among five components: goals, conceptual framework, research questions, methods, and validity.

### Key Qualitative Design Traditions

**Phenomenology**
: Explores the *essence* of lived experience of a phenomenon (Moustakas, 1994). Requires careful bracketing of researcher assumptions. Typically involves 5–25 participants who have all experienced the phenomenon.

**Grounded Theory**
: Generates theory grounded in systematically gathered and analyzed data (Charmaz, 2006; Glaser & Strauss, 1967). Coding moves from initial to focused to theoretical. Sampling continues until theoretical saturation.

**Ethnography**
: Describes and interprets a culture-sharing group through prolonged engagement (Emerson, Fretz, & Shaw, 2011). Requires extended fieldwork — often months to years of observation and participation.

**Case Study**
: In-depth investigation of a bounded system — a person, program, event, or community (Merriam & Tisdell, 2016). Can be single or multiple-case. Requires clear definition of the "case" and its boundaries.

**Narrative Inquiry**
: Studies the stories people tell about their experiences, analyzing structure, content, and performance of narratives. Often focuses on one or a small number of individuals.

## Quantitative Research Designs

Shadish, Cook, and Campbell (2002) provide the definitive framework for understanding quantitative designs in terms of *threats to validity*:

**True experiments**
: Random assignment to conditions. The gold standard for internal validity but often impractical in social science and LIS research.

**Quasi-experiments**
: No random assignment but still involve intervention/comparison. Requires careful attention to selection threats and alternative explanations.

**Correlational / Survey designs**
: Examine relationships among variables without manipulation. Cannot establish causation but useful for description and prediction. Dillman et al. (2016) provide the standard reference for survey methodology.

## Mixed Methods Designs

Creswell and Creswell (2023, Ch. 10) describe three core designs:

### Convergent Design
Collect QUAL and QUAN data simultaneously, analyze separately, then merge for comparison. The classic triangulation design. Works well when you want to compare or corroborate findings from different data sources.

### Explanatory Sequential Design
QUAN data collection and analysis → QUAL follow-up to explain or elaborate. Often used when quantitative results need deeper context. The QUAL strand is designed *after* seeing QUAN results.

### Exploratory Sequential Design
QUAL data collection and analysis → QUAN instrument development or testing. Used when existing instruments are inadequate for your population or construct. The QUAL strand informs the QUAN design.

```{important}
The key challenge in mixed methods is **integration** — the point at which qualitative and quantitative strands inform each other. Simply collecting both types of data is not mixed methods research; integration must be deliberate and visible.

Fetters, Curry, and Creswell (2013) identify integration at three levels: design, methods, and interpretation/reporting.
```
