# Philosophical Foundations of Inquiry

## Why Philosophy Matters in Dissertation Research

Every methodological choice you make — from how you frame your research questions to how you analyze data — rests on deeper assumptions about the nature of reality (ontology), how we can know things about that reality (epistemology), and what values inform the inquiry (axiology). These assumptions are not merely academic exercises; they form the *logic of justification* for your entire study.

As Creswell and Creswell (2023) argue, researchers should make the larger philosophical ideas they espouse explicit, because this information explains *why* they chose a particular approach. Guba (1990) defined a worldview as "a basic set of beliefs that guide action" (p. 17). Others have used the terms *paradigms* (Lincoln, Lynham, & Guba, 2011), *epistemologies and ontologies* (Crotty, 1998), or *broadly conceived research methodologies* (Neuman, 2009).

```{important}
A dissertation committee will probe your philosophical foundations. Your methodology chapter must articulate these assumptions clearly and show how they logically connect to your design choices.
```

## Four Major Worldviews

The contemporary literature identifies four widely discussed philosophical orientations. Each carries distinct implications for how you design and conduct your study.

### Postpositivism

Rooted in the 19th-century work of Comte, Mill, and Durkheim, and refined by Phillips and Burbules (2000), postpositivism represents the *thinking after positivism* — acknowledging that absolute certainty about human behavior is unattainable while still pursuing objective measurement and causal explanation.

**Core tenets:**
- Knowledge is *conjectural* — we indicate a failure to reject a hypothesis, never "prove" one
- Research proceeds through *theory verification*: begin with a theory, collect data, refine
- Objectivity, reductionism, and careful measurement are paramount
- Standards of validity and reliability discipline the inquiry

**Methodological alignment**: Experiments, quasi-experiments, survey research, correlational designs.

### Constructivism (Social Constructivism / Interpretivism)

Drawing on Berger and Luckmann (1967) and Lincoln and Guba (1985), constructivists hold that individuals construct subjective meanings of their experiences. The researcher's goal is to understand this *complexity of views* rather than narrow them into predetermined categories.

**Core tenets:**
- Multiple, socially and historically constructed realities
- Researcher and participant co-construct understanding
- Theory *emerges* from data rather than being tested against it
- Context is essential; findings are situated, not universal

**Methodological alignment**: Phenomenology, grounded theory, ethnography, case study, narrative inquiry.

### Transformative

Emerging from critical theory, feminist research, and participatory action paradigms, the transformative worldview centers issues of power, oppression, and social justice.

**Core tenets:**
- Research is inherently political
- Marginalized voices must be centered
- The inquiry should challenge existing power structures
- Collaboration between researcher and community is essential

**Methodological alignment**: Participatory action research, community-based research, critical discourse analysis.

### Pragmatism

Originating with Peirce, James, Mead, and Dewey, pragmatism focuses on *consequences of actions* and *what works* to answer the research question. It provides the most common philosophical justification for mixed methods research.

**Core tenets:**
- Not committed to any one philosophical system
- Research problem drives method selection, not the other way around
- Truth is provisional — what works at the time
- Pluralistic: multiple methods and data sources are legitimate

**Methodological alignment**: Mixed methods designs (convergent, explanatory sequential, exploratory sequential).

```{admonition} Comparing Worldviews Across Philosophical Dimensions
:class: note

| Dimension | Postpositivism | Constructivism | Transformative | Pragmatism |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|----------------|------------|
| **Ontology** | Single apprehendable reality | Multiple co-constructed realities | Reality shaped by power | What works in practice |
| **Epistemology** | Objectivist (detached) | Subjectivist (co-created) | Collaborative | Pluralistic |
| **Axiology** | Value-free (bias controlled) | Value-laden (acknowledged) | Value-driven (justice) | Value-aware (problem-centered) |
| **Methodology** | Deductive (hypothesis testing) | Inductive (emergent) | Participatory (action) | Abductive (mixed) |

```

## The Paradigm–Methodology Connection

Crotty (1998) offered a foundational four-level hierarchy connecting philosophical commitments to concrete research actions:

1. **Epistemology** (objectivism, constructionism, subjectivism)
2. **Theoretical perspective** (positivism, interpretivism, critical theory, feminism)
3. **Methodology** (experimental, survey, ethnography, grounded theory)
4. **Methods** (questionnaires, interviews, observation, statistical analysis)

As Anfara and Mertz (2015) emphasize, these levels *inform one another* — an interpretivist epistemology does not logically lead to experimental methodology. Understanding this hierarchy helps you build a coherent, defensible research design where every choice aligns with every other.

Denzin and Lincoln (2013) argued that paradigms are "overarching philosophical systems denoting particular ontologies, epistemologies, and methodologies... [which] represent belief systems that attach the user to a particular worldview" (p. 11). The researcher "approaches the world with a set of ideas, a framework (theory, ontology) that specifies a set of questions (epistemology) that he or she then examines in specific ways (methodology, analysis)" (2003b, p. 30).

```{warning}
A common dissertation weakness is claiming a constructivist epistemology while using language and methods that presuppose postpositivist assumptions. Your worldview section need not be lengthy, but it must be *present* and *coherent*.
```
