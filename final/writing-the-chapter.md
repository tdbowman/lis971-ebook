# Writing the Methodology Chapter

## The Chapter as an Argument

Your methodology chapter is not a procedures manual-it is an *extended argument* for why your approach is the best available way to answer your research questions. Every section should convey not just *what* you will do but *why*.

A strong methodology chapter demonstrates three things to your committee:

1. **Coherence**: Every piece fits together-worldview, design, sampling, data collection, analysis, and quality criteria form a unified logic
2. **Rigor**: You understand and will apply the quality standards appropriate to your research tradition
3. **Feasibility**: You can actually execute this plan within your timeline and resources

---

## Recommended Structure

A well-organized methodology chapter typically follows this sequence (adapted from Creswell & Creswell, 2023):

### 1. Introduction and Overview (1 paragraph)

Restate the purpose and research questions. Preview the methodological approach. This orients the reader and provides a roadmap.

### 2. Philosophical Assumptions (0.5–1 page)

Your worldview, epistemological stance, and how these shape the design. See {doc}`../block1/philosophical-foundations`.

### 3. Research Design (1–2 pages)

Design type, rationale, and visual diagram (especially for mixed methods). See {doc}`../block1/research-design`.

### 4. Population and Sampling (1–2 pages)

Population definition, sampling strategy, size justification, recruitment procedures, and inclusion/exclusion criteria. See {doc}`../block1/sampling-strategies`.

### 5. Data Collection Procedures (2–3 pages)

Detailed protocols for each method. Instruments should be summarized here and included in full as appendices. See {doc}`../block2/qualitative-data-collection` and {doc}`../block2/quantitative-instruments`.

### 6. Data Analysis Plan (2–3 pages)

Step-by-step procedures, software, and integration strategy (if mixed methods). See {doc}`../block3/qualitative-analysis`, {doc}`../block3/quantitative-analysis`, and {doc}`../block3/mixed-methods-integration`.

### 7. Validity / Trustworthiness (1–2 pages)

Quality criteria appropriate to your tradition, with specifics about how each strategy will operate in your study.

### 8. Ethical Considerations (0.5–1 page)

IRB status, consent, confidentiality, risk mitigation. See {doc}`../block2/digital-methods-ethics`.

### 9. Limitations (0.5–1 page)

Known constraints and how you will address them. Every study has limitations; acknowledging them demonstrates methodological sophistication, not weakness.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

```{warning}
These are the most frequent weaknesses in dissertation methodology chapters. Review your draft against each one before submitting.
```

**Inconsistent worldview**
: Claiming constructivism but writing in postpositivist language (e.g., "objective data," "unbiased findings"). Ensure your language matches your stated epistemological position.

**Vague sampling**
: "I will interview some people" is not a sampling strategy. Specify whom, how many, why them, and how you will recruit them.

**Missing justifications**
: Describing procedures without explaining *why* they are appropriate for your research questions and tradition. Every method needs a rationale.

**Orphaned methods**
: Using a data collection method or analytic procedure that is not connected to any research question. If it doesn't answer an RQ, why are you doing it?

**Generic quality criteria**
: Listing trustworthiness strategies (e.g., "I will use triangulation and member checking") without explaining *specifically* how they will operate in your study.

**Ethics as afterthought**
: A two-sentence paragraph about IRB approval is insufficient. Demonstrate that ethical reasoning has been woven into your design from the start.

**No limitations**
: Every study has limitations. Acknowledging them shows maturity and awareness, not weakness.

**Unsupported claims**
: Methodological choices must be supported by methodological literature. If you claim thematic analysis is appropriate, cite Braun and Clarke (2006), not just your topical literature.

## The Revision Memo

After each checkpoint weekend, you will submit a revision memo. This document is a crucial professional skill-the ability to receive and integrate criticism constructively.

### Revision Memo Structure

1. **Summary of feedback received** (from peers, instructor, and self-assessment)
2. **Changes made in response**-be specific: "I revised my sampling section to include a power analysis following Cohen's (1992) framework"
3. **Feedback not incorporated** and your rationale for that decision-it is legitimate to disagree with feedback if you can justify your reasoning
4. **Remaining questions or concerns**-what are you still unsure about?

```{tip}
The revision memo demonstrates your ability to engage critically with feedback. Committees value this skill because it shows you can navigate the iterative process of dissertation revision that lies ahead.
```
