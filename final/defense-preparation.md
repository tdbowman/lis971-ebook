# Preparing for the Defense

Your methodology chapter will eventually be part of your dissertation proposal defense. Preparing for tough questions now — while you're still designing your study — is far better than discovering weaknesses after your committee reads the proposal.

---

## What Committees Probe

Dissertation committees evaluate methodology chapters on five dimensions:

### Coherence
> *Does every piece fit together?*

- Do your methods actually answer your research questions?
- Does your worldview align with your design and analysis approach?
- Is there a logical thread from philosophy → design → sampling → collection → analysis?

### Rigor
> *Have you demonstrated mastery of your tradition's quality standards?*

- Are your quality criteria appropriate to your methodology (not borrowed from another tradition)?
- Have you addressed the specific threats to validity or trustworthiness that your design faces?
- Is your sampling logic sound, with appropriate justification for size and strategy?

### Feasibility
> *Can you actually do this?*

- Is your timeline realistic for the scope of data collection you propose?
- Do you have access to the participants, sites, or data you need?
- Do you have the methodological skills to execute your analysis plan?

### Ethics
> *Have you thought carefully about risks and protections?*

- Is your informed consent process appropriate for your population?
- Have you considered the ethical dimensions specific to your study context?
- Does your data management plan adequately protect participants?

### Flexibility
> *What happens if something goes wrong?*

- What is your contingency plan if you can't recruit enough participants?
- What will you do if an instrument has poor reliability with your sample?
- How will you handle unexpected findings or access barriers?

## Common Committee Questions

Prepare answers for these frequently asked questions:

**On design choice:**
- "Why did you choose [this design] rather than [alternative]?"
- "What would you lose by using a different approach?"
- "How does your design address [specific threat to validity]?"

**On sampling:**
- "How do you know [N] participants will be enough?"
- "What will you do if you can't reach saturation / your target N?"
- "How will you address potential selection bias in your sample?"

**On data collection:**
- "Walk me through exactly what a data collection session looks like."
- "How will you handle [specific practical challenge]?"
- "What training or piloting will you do before full data collection?"

**On analysis:**
- "Why this analytic approach rather than [alternative]?"
- "How will you handle data that doesn't fit your codes/themes/model?"
- "What software will you use, and are you proficient with it?"

**On integration (mixed methods):**
- "At what specific point(s) do your QUAL and QUAN strands connect?"
- "What will you do if the two strands produce contradictory findings?"
- "Why do you need both methods? What does mixing add?"

**On limitations:**
- "What are the biggest threats to the quality of your findings?"
- "How generalizable / transferable are your expected results?"
- "What can your study NOT tell us?"

## Building Your Defense

```{tip}
For each question above that applies to your study, write a 2–3 sentence response. Keep these in a "defense preparation" document that you update as your methodology evolves. By proposal defense time, you'll have a ready reference for the most common challenges.
```

### The "So What?" Test

For every methodological decision, ask yourself:

1. **What** am I doing?
2. **Why** am I doing it this way?
3. **What** would happen if I did it differently?
4. **What** evidence (methodological literature) supports my choice?

If you can answer all four for every major decision in your methodology chapter, you are well-prepared for your defense.

## ✏️ Exercise: Mock Defense Preparation

1. Identify the **three weakest points** in your current methodology chapter draft
2. For each, write out the question a committee member might ask and your best response
3. Share these with your accountability triad and ask them to challenge your responses
4. Revise your methodology chapter to address the weaknesses proactively
